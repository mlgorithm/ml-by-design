\chapter*{Preface}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Preface}

Real model-based AI work begins with a problem: what decision is being supported, what evidence is available, what claim is being made, and what would justify trusting the result?

This book is organized around that habit. It is problem-driven rather than algorithm-driven. Models, losses, architectures, and systems appear because they answer modeling dilemmas. Each method is introduced through the decision, data, representation, evidence, and system constraints that make it useful.

The book is written for undergraduate students, teachers, and serious self-learners who want a coherent first serious introduction to machine learning, evaluation, and reliable AI systems. It assumes curiosity, some programming experience, and enough mathematical maturity to follow equations when they are explained carefully. Two mathematical bridge chapters review linear algebra and probability, risk, and learning signals. Most first-course readers should begin with Chapter 1 and use those bridges as just-in-time review.

Because the audience is mixed, the manuscript uses a simple depth convention. The \textbf{core path} is the material needed for a first serious reading and for the chapter artifact. \textbf{Extension} or \textbf{Advanced Note} material gives contest, project, instructor, or second-pass depth. Instructors may assign those sections selectively; self-learners should not treat every advanced box as a prerequisite for continuing.

The center of gravity is modern machine learning---including probabilistic models, generative models, representation learning, recommendation systems, reinforcement learning, and the system habits needed to evaluate, stress-test, and operationalize model-based systems.

\section*{Suggested Reading Paths}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{First machine-learning course.} Start with Chapter 1, continue in order, and use the Mathematical Bridge chapters when later chapters point back to them or when notation starts to slow you down.
\item \textbf{Math-refresh path.} Read Chapter 1 for the problem-framing spine, then work through the two Mathematical Bridge chapters before continuing to Chapter 2.
\item \textbf{One-semester teaching path.} Use Chapters~\ref{ch:framing-learning-problems}--\ref{ch:dataset-design} as the core. Add Chapter~\ref{ch:optimization-neural-networks}, Chapter~\ref{ch:probabilistic-models} if probabilistic foundations matter, Chapter~\ref{ch:representation-foundation-models}, one modality chapter from Chapters~\ref{ch:vision}--\ref{ch:audio-time}, Chapter~\ref{ch:ranking-recommendation} if ranking is relevant, and Chapters~\ref{ch:experiments-claims}--\ref{ch:models-systems} for experiments, reliability, and systems.
\item \textbf{Probabilistic and generative track.} Follow Bridge~II, Chapter~\ref{ch:structure-beyond-linearity}, Chapter~\ref{ch:probabilistic-models}, Chapter~\ref{ch:representation-foundation-models}, Chapter~\ref{ch:generative-representations}, and Chapter~\ref{ch:reliability}.
\end{itemize}

Every serious AI project eventually has to answer seven questions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=\arabic*.]
\item What decision are we actually supporting?
\item What learning task is the closest defensible proxy for that decision?
\item What data stands in for the world, and what could bias or leak it?
\item What representation makes the relevant structure visible?
\item What is the simplest serious baseline?
\item What evidence would justify the claim?
\item How will the model output become an action inside a real system?
\end{enumerate}

Chapters 1 through 3 introduce this method through task framing, predictive judgment, and evaluation discipline. Later chapters extend the same logic to optimization, neural networks, probabilistic models, representations, generative models, modalities, recommendation, experiments, reliability, and systems. Deployment becomes part of the modeling problem.

The book returns to a few running settings so that the method has continuity. Course support examples ground framing, metric choice, baselines, retrieval, escalation, and deployment. A dashboard-camera pedestrian detector carries similar questions into vision and system reliability. Across these settings, the recurring techniques are decision-first framing, proxy audit, representation audit, baseline ladder, claim audit, slice diagnosis, threshold as policy, pipeline realism, reuse audit, invariance audit, context audit, and prediction-time audit.

The chapters also use recurring teaching devices: a \textbf{Problem-Solving Lens} to name the modeling dilemma, a \textbf{Problem-Solving Technique} to make a reusable reasoning move explicit, a \textbf{Math Lens} to explain the mechanism, a \textbf{From Scratch} fragment to expose implementation details, a \textbf{Failure Pattern} to show how reasonable choices mislead, and a \textbf{Chapter Artifact} to turn the reasoning into a memo, card, sheet, or brief.

The companion code carries the larger implementations. The text emphasizes concepts, notation, pseudocode, and durable examples so that the ideas stay useful as library APIs change. Each chapter follows a common rhythm: a modeling dilemma, worked examples, reusable teaching devices, failure modes, and exercises that strengthen four habits of mind: checking concepts, building small solutions, diagnosing failure, and designing better systems. The goal is to make rigor usable.
