\chapter{Generative Representations}
\label{ch:generative-representations}

The previous chapter treated representations as tools for \emph{understanding}---spaces in which downstream tasks become easier. But a representation can also be used to \emph{generate}: to produce new data points that look like the training data but were never observed. A course-support system that generates realistic synthetic student messages can augment scarce labeled data. A pedestrian detector that generates novel nighttime scenes can fill gaps in its training distribution. And the same latent spaces that enable generation also enable interpolation, anomaly detection, and controllable editing.

This chapter introduces three families of generative models---variational autoencoders, generative adversarial networks, and diffusion models---each of which learns a latent representation from which new data can be sampled \citep{kingma2014vae,goodfellow2014gan,ho2020ddpm}. The probabilistic foundations from Chapter~\ref{ch:probabilistic-models} motivate VAEs and latent-variable sampling, while GANs introduce adversarial training. The practical evaluation challenge---how do you know if generated data is any good?---runs through all three.

\begin{problemsolvinglens}
Generation is not an end in itself. Every generative model encodes assumptions about what variation matters and what should be preserved. The design question is not ``can the model generate?'' but ``does what it generates serve the downstream task, and how would we know?''
\end{problemsolvinglens}

\begin{thinkingtool}
\textbf{Representation for generation.} When evaluating a generative model, ask: what does the latent space \emph{mean}? Can you move in the latent space and predict what changes in the output? If you cannot, the model may still generalize, but it is harder to trust the latent space for controlled generation or interpretation.
\end{thinkingtool}

\begin{learninggoals}
\item Explain what makes a learned latent space usable for generation.
\item Describe the VAE objective as reconstruction plus regularization of the latent distribution.
\item Compare VAEs, GANs, diffusion models, and flows by their design trade-offs.
\item Identify mode collapse, weak diversity, and synthetic-data contamination as downstream risks.
\item Treat FID, Inception Score, and human evaluation as imperfect evidence rather than definitive proof.
\item Decide when generated data should be judged by downstream task performance.
\item Construct a generative model audit card documenting what the model generates, what it misses, and what failure modes to watch for.
\end{learninggoals}

\begin{practicalnote}
\textbf{Depth guide.} In a first course, the core expectation is to compare generative families, explain their objectives at a design level, and evaluate their downstream risks. Training stable GANs, deriving diffusion objectives in full, or building a production generator belongs in an extended project.
\end{practicalnote}

\section{From Representation to Generation}

Chapter~\ref{ch:representation-foundation-models} introduced autoencoders: networks that compress an input into a bottleneck representation and then reconstruct it. The bottleneck forces the network to learn a compact representation that preserves the most important structure. But a standard autoencoder's latent space has no particular structure---it is not organized in a way that supports sampling new points. If you pick a random point in the latent space of a trained autoencoder and decode it, the result is usually meaningless noise.

Generative models impose structure on the latent space so that it \emph{can} be sampled. The three families differ in how they achieve this:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Variational autoencoders (VAEs)} regularize the latent space to be a smooth, continuous distribution from which new points can be sampled and decoded into realistic outputs.
\item \emph{Generative adversarial networks (GANs)} learn a mapping from a simple random distribution to realistic outputs by training a generator to fool a discriminator.
\item \emph{Diffusion models} learn to reverse a gradual noising process, generating data by starting from pure noise and iteratively denoising.
\end{itemize}

Each trades off sample quality, diversity, training stability, and computational cost differently.

\section{Variational Autoencoders}
\label{sec:vae}

\subsection{The Idea: Structured Latent Spaces}

A variational autoencoder (VAE) modifies the standard autoencoder in one key way: the encoder does not map an input to a single latent point, but to a \emph{distribution} over latent points. Specifically, the encoder usually outputs the mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and per-coordinate variance $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2$ of a diagonal Gaussian distribution in latent space. A latent point is then \emph{sampled} from this distribution and passed to the decoder.

This seemingly small change has a large consequence: the latent space becomes a smooth, continuous probability distribution. Nearby points in latent space map to similar outputs. Interpolating between two latent codes produces a gradual transition between the corresponding outputs. And new data can be generated by sampling from the prior distribution over the latent space.

\subsection{The VAE Objective}

The VAE is trained to optimize a single objective that balances two goals:

\textbf{Reconstruction.} The decoded output should be close to the original input. This is measured by a reconstruction loss (mean squared error for continuous data, cross-entropy for discrete data), just like a standard autoencoder.

\textbf{Regularization.} The encoder's output distribution should stay close to a simple prior---typically a standard Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$. This is measured by the KL divergence between the encoder's output distribution and the prior:
\[
D_{\text{KL}}\bigl(q(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}) \;\|\; p(\mathbf{z})\bigr)
\]
where $q(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}(\mathbf{x}), \operatorname{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2(\mathbf{x})))$ is the encoder's output and $p(\mathbf{z}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$ is the prior.

The total loss is:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{VAE}} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})}\bigl[-\log p(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z})\bigr]}_{\text{reconstruction}} + \underbrace{D_{\text{KL}}\bigl(q(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}) \;\|\; p(\mathbf{z})\bigr)}_{\text{regularization}}
\]

This is the negative \emph{evidence lower bound} (ELBO) used in variational autoencoders \citep{kingma2014vae,rezende2014sgvb}. Minimizing it simultaneously improves reconstruction and keeps the latent space well-organized.

\begin{practicalnote}
The balance between reconstruction and regularization is controlled by a weighting factor $\beta$ (the ``$\beta$-VAE'' variant). With $\beta = 1$, the objective is the standard ELBO; the two terms are not necessarily equal in scale or contribution. Increasing $\beta$ increases the relative KL pressure and can produce a more structured, disentangled latent space but blurrier reconstructions. Decreasing $\beta$ reduces that pressure and can produce sharper reconstructions but a less organized latent space. In practice, tuning $\beta$ is often necessary to get useful results for the downstream task.
\end{practicalnote}

\subsection{The Reparameterization Trick}

Training a VAE requires backpropagating gradients through the sampling step---but sampling is not a differentiable operation. The \emph{reparameterization trick} resolves this by rewriting the sampling as a deterministic transformation of a noise variable:
\[
\mathbf{z} = \boldsymbol{\mu}(\mathbf{x}) + \boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{x}) \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})
\]

The randomness is isolated in $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$, which does not depend on the model parameters. Gradients can now flow through $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ to the encoder. This trick is what makes VAE training practical with standard backpropagation.

\subsection{What VAEs Are Good For}

\textbf{Latent interpolation.} Given two inputs $\mathbf{x}_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_2$, encode them and interpolate between their posterior means, for example $\mathbf{z}_t = (1-t)\boldsymbol{\mu}(\mathbf{x}_1) + t\boldsymbol{\mu}(\mathbf{x}_2)$, then decode. A well-trained VAE produces smooth transitions: morphing between two faces, transitioning between writing styles, or blending pedestrian poses.

\textbf{Anomaly detection.} Inputs that are unlike the training data may have high reconstruction error, low model likelihood or ELBO, or an unusual posterior relative to the learned aggregate posterior. This can make VAEs useful for detecting out-of-distribution inputs, but the score must be validated for the deployment domain---a capability that connects directly to the epistemic uncertainty discussion in Chapter~\ref{ch:probabilistic-models}.

\textbf{Data augmentation.} Sample from the latent space to generate new training examples. Unlike rule-based augmentation (Chapter~\ref{ch:dataset-design}), VAE-based augmentation generates novel examples rather than transforming existing ones. But the same validation discipline applies: augmented data must be evaluated against a real held-out test set.

\begin{example}[VAE for Student Message Augmentation]
The course-support team has 200 labeled URGENT messages but needs more to train a reliable classifier. They train a VAE on all 12,000 student messages (ignoring labels). The VAE learns a latent space where similar messages are nearby. The team encodes the 200 URGENT messages, samples new latent points near their encodings (adding small noise), and decodes them. The generated messages resemble real URGENT messages but are not copies. After manual inspection to remove implausible generations, the team adds 150 synthetic URGENT messages to the training set. The classifier's recall on URGENT messages improves from 0.71 to 0.79 on the held-out gold test set.
\end{example}

\begin{warningbox}
VAE-generated samples tend to be blurrier than real data, especially for images. This is a consequence of the Gaussian decoder assumption and the reconstruction loss, which averages over possible outputs. For tasks where sharp, realistic detail matters (e.g., generating training images for a pedestrian detector), GANs or diffusion models may produce better samples. For tasks where diversity and coverage matter more than sharpness (e.g., generating text variations), VAEs are often sufficient.
\end{warningbox}

\section{Generative Adversarial Networks}
\label{sec:gan}

\subsection{The Adversarial Idea}

Generative adversarial networks (GANs) take a fundamentally different approach to generation. Instead of learning a latent distribution explicitly, a GAN learns to generate data through a two-player game:

\textbf{The generator} $G$ takes a random noise vector $\mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$ and produces a synthetic data point $G(\mathbf{z})$.

\textbf{The discriminator} $D$ takes a data point (either real or generated) and outputs the probability that it is real.

The two networks are trained simultaneously with opposing objectives. The discriminator tries to correctly distinguish real from generated data. The generator tries to produce data that fools the discriminator. Formally:
\[
\min_G \max_D \; \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log D(\mathbf{x})] + \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z} \sim p(\mathbf{z})}[\log(1 - D(G(\mathbf{z})))]
\]
This is the original minimax GAN objective from \citet{goodfellow2014gan}; many practical GAN variants modify the loss or regularization to improve stability.

At equilibrium (in theory), the generator produces data indistinguishable from real data, and the discriminator outputs 0.5 for everything.

\subsection{Why GANs Produce Sharp Samples}

Unlike VAEs, which optimize a reconstruction loss that averages over uncertainty (producing blurry outputs), GANs are trained by a discriminator that penalizes any detectable difference from real data. This adversarial pressure pushes the generator toward sharp, realistic outputs. For image generation, this difference is visually striking: GAN-generated faces can be nearly photorealistic, while VAE-generated faces are typically smoother and less detailed.

\subsection{Training Instability}

The adversarial training procedure is inherently unstable. The generator and discriminator must stay in balance: if the discriminator becomes too strong, the generator receives no useful gradient signal (it is always detected, so all generated samples look equally bad). If the generator becomes too strong too quickly, the discriminator fails to provide meaningful feedback. Common failure modes include:

\textbf{Mode collapse.} The generator learns to produce only a few types of outputs that fool the discriminator, ignoring the diversity of the real data. A face-generating GAN might produce only young female faces; a pedestrian-generating GAN might produce only frontal-view standing poses. The discriminator sees realistic samples but cannot detect that the generator's output lacks diversity.

\textbf{Oscillation.} The generator and discriminator cycle: the generator finds an exploit, the discriminator catches on, the generator switches to a new exploit. Training loss oscillates without converging.

\textbf{Vanishing gradients.} If the discriminator is too confident (outputs near 0 or 1), the gradient signal to the generator vanishes, stalling training.

\begin{warningbox}
Mode collapse is insidious because standard training metrics may not detect it. The discriminator loss may converge, the generator loss may decrease, and individual samples may look realistic---but the \emph{distribution} of generated samples may cover only a fraction of the real distribution. Always inspect the diversity of generated samples, not just their quality.
\end{warningbox}

\begin{practicalnote}
Several techniques improve GAN training stability:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Wasserstein GAN (WGAN):} Replaces the original objective with the Wasserstein distance, which provides smoother gradients even when the discriminator is strong.
\item \emph{Spectral normalization:} Constrains the discriminator's Lipschitz constant, preventing it from becoming too sharp.
\item \emph{Progressive growing:} Starts training at low resolution and gradually increases, giving the generator time to learn coarse structure before fine detail.
\item \emph{Two-timescale learning rates:} Uses a higher learning rate for the discriminator, keeping it ahead of the generator just enough to provide useful gradients.
\end{itemize}
None of these fully solves the stability problem. GAN training requires more monitoring and hyperparameter tuning than standard supervised training.
\end{practicalnote}

\subsection{Conditional Generation}

A \emph{conditional GAN} (cGAN) adds a conditioning input---a class label, a text description, or an image---so the generator produces outputs that match the condition. The generator becomes $G(\mathbf{z}, \mathbf{c})$ and the discriminator becomes $D(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{c})$, where $\mathbf{c}$ is the condition.

\begin{example}[Conditional GAN for Pedestrian Scenes]
A pedestrian detection team wants to augment its training data with nighttime scenes (an epistemic gap identified in the uncertainty audit). They train a conditional GAN where the condition is ``time of day'' (day, dusk, night). The generator learns to produce synthetic pedestrian scenes conditioned on lighting. Given the condition ``night,'' it generates dark scenes with streetlight illumination and pedestrian silhouettes. The synthetic nighttime scenes are added to the training set, and the detector's nighttime recall improves from 0.62 to 0.74 on real nighttime test frames. The gap between synthetic and real is confirmed by separate evaluation on the real test set.
\end{example}

\section{Diffusion Models}
\label{sec:diffusion}

\subsection{The Denoising Idea}

Diffusion models are a major current family in image and media generation, building on denoising diffusion and score-based generative modeling \citep{ho2020ddpm,song2021score}. Their core idea is surprisingly simple: learn to reverse a gradual noising process.

\textbf{Forward process (noising).} Start with a real data point $\mathbf{x}_0$. Over $T$ steps, add small amounts of Gaussian noise, producing a sequence $\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_T$. At $T$ large enough, $\mathbf{x}_T$ is indistinguishable from pure Gaussian noise.

\textbf{Reverse process (denoising).} Train a neural network $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)$ to predict the noise that was added at each step. To generate new data, start from pure noise $\mathbf{x}_T \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$ and iteratively denoise: at each step, use the network to predict and remove the noise, gradually revealing a realistic data point.

The training objective is simple: minimize the mean squared error between the predicted noise and the actual noise added at each step:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{diffusion}} = \mathbb{E}_{t, \mathbf{x}_0, \boldsymbol{\epsilon}}\bigl[\|\boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)\|^2\bigr]
\]

\subsection{Why Diffusion Models Work Well}

Diffusion models have several advantages over VAEs and GANs:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Training stability.} The training objective is a regression loss (predict the noise), with no adversarial dynamics. This often makes optimization easier to monitor than GAN training, though architecture, noise schedule, data scale, and compute still matter.
\item \emph{Sample quality.} By decomposing generation into many small denoising steps, diffusion models can produce extremely detailed, high-quality outputs. Each step makes a small correction, avoiding the single-shot generation challenge of GANs and VAEs.
\item \emph{Mode coverage.} Diffusion models are often less prone to the classic GAN failure mode where a generator collapses to a few sample types, but coverage is still an empirical question. Rare cases, underrepresented slices, and conditioning failures must be checked directly.
\end{itemize}

The main disadvantage is computational cost. Generation requires running the denoising network for hundreds or thousands of steps (one forward pass per step), making generation much slower than a single-pass GAN or VAE decoder.

\begin{practicalnote}
Accelerated sampling methods such as DDIM and DPM-Solver reduce the number of denoising steps needed, trading some quality for speed \citep{song2020ddim,lu2022dpmsolver}. In practice, tens of sampling steps may suffice for many uses, compared with the original 1000-step diffusion schedule. During training, implementations usually sample timesteps from that schedule rather than unrolling every denoising step for every example. For applications where generation speed matters, such as real-time augmentation during training, this trade-off is important.
\end{practicalnote}

\subsection{Guidance: Controlling What Gets Generated}

Diffusion models can be \emph{guided} toward specific outputs using a conditioning signal. \emph{Classifier-free guidance} trains the model both with and without the condition, then amplifies the difference at generation time \citep{ho2022classifierfree}. A guidance scale $w > 1$ pushes the output more strongly toward the condition:
\[
\hat{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}} = \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, \varnothing) + w \cdot \bigl(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, \mathbf{c}) - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, \varnothing)\bigr)
\]

Higher guidance produces samples that match the condition more faithfully but with less diversity. This is the \emph{quality--diversity trade-off}: strong guidance gives you exactly what you asked for, but the outputs become repetitive. Weak guidance gives diverse outputs that may not match the condition well.

\begin{example}[Diffusion for Synthetic Training Data]
A team building a pedestrian detector needs training images of ``pedestrian crossing a wet road at dusk.'' A text-conditioned diffusion model generates 500 such images. With low guidance ($w=2$), the images are diverse---some show the road clearly, others are more abstract---but not all are realistic enough for training. With high guidance ($w=10$), the images are more uniform and realistic, but they lack diversity (similar poses, similar lighting). The team uses medium guidance ($w=5$) and post-filters the outputs, keeping 300 images that pass a visual quality check. These are evaluated against real test frames to measure the sim-to-real gap.
\end{example}

\subsection{Brief Note: Normalizing Flows and Flow Matching}

Two other generative approaches deserve mention. \emph{Normalizing flows} define an explicit, invertible mapping between a simple distribution (Gaussian) and the data distribution \citep{dinh2017realnvp}. Because the mapping is invertible, exact likelihood computation is possible---unlike VAEs (which use a lower bound) or GANs (which have no likelihood). The constraint is that all transformations must be invertible and have tractable Jacobians, which limits architectural flexibility.

\emph{Flow matching} relaxes this constraint by defining a continuous-time flow from noise to data, trained with a regression objective related to diffusion and continuous-normalizing-flow views \citep{lipman2023flowmatching}. Sampling still typically requires numerically integrating that learned flow over multiple solver steps, but in practice it can use fewer or cheaper steps than standard diffusion sampling while maintaining high sample quality. Flow matching is an active area of research and should be treated as a fast-moving family rather than as a settled recipe.

Both methods share the theme of this chapter: learning a mapping between a simple latent distribution and a complex data distribution, with the latent space providing structure for generation and interpolation.

\section{Evaluating Generative Models}
\label{sec:gen-eval}

Evaluating generative models is fundamentally harder than evaluating classifiers. A classifier's output can be compared to a ground-truth label. A generative model's output is a new data point---there is no single ``correct'' answer. Evaluation must assess two properties simultaneously: \emph{quality} (do individual samples look realistic?) and \emph{diversity} (does the model produce the full range of variation in the data?).

\subsection{Automated Metrics}

\textbf{Fr\'{e}chet Inception Distance (FID).} A widely used metric for image generation \citep{heusel2017fid}. FID measures the distance between the distribution of real images and the distribution of generated images in the feature space of a pre-trained Inception network. Lower FID means the generated distribution is closer to the real distribution in that feature space. FID captures both quality (unrealistic images have unusual features) and diversity (a mode-collapsed generator will have a narrower feature distribution), but only through the chosen representation.

\textbf{Inception Score (IS).} Measures two properties: each generated image should be confidently classified by an Inception network (quality), and the marginal distribution of predicted labels across generated images should have high entropy (diversity) \citep{salimans2016improved}. Higher IS is better under that proxy. IS has known limitations: it does not compare to the real data distribution, its label-diversity assumption may not match a single-domain dataset, and it can be gamed by producing one very realistic image per predicted class.

\textbf{Precision and Recall for generation.} Adapted from classification, \emph{precision} estimates how much generated mass lies near the real data distribution (quality), and \emph{recall} estimates how much of the real data distribution is covered by generated samples (diversity) \citep{kynkaanniemi2019pr}. A mode-collapsed GAN can have high precision but low recall.

\begin{warningbox}
All automated metrics for generative models are proxies. FID depends on the choice of feature extractor (Inception was trained on ImageNet, not on your domain). IS does not compare to the real distribution. Precision-recall requires approximate support estimation in feature space, so its result depends on the representation and neighborhood method used. No single number captures whether generated data is ``good enough'' for a given downstream task. Always complement automated metrics with visual inspection and, when possible, downstream task performance.
\end{warningbox}

\subsection{Human Evaluation}

For many applications, human evaluation remains essential. Common protocols include:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Turing test / real-vs-fake.} Human raters are shown a mix of real and generated samples and asked to identify which are real. The fooling rate measures quality.
\item \emph{Preference ranking.} Human raters compare outputs from two or more models and state which they prefer. This is useful for comparing models but does not measure absolute quality.
\item \emph{Task-specific evaluation.} If generated data is used for augmentation, the downstream task metric (e.g., classification accuracy on real test data) is the ultimate evaluation. A generative model that produces beautiful images that do not help the downstream task is not useful.
\end{itemize}

\begin{practicalnote}
When generated data is used for training (data augmentation or synthetic training), the only evaluation that ultimately matters is downstream performance on a held-out \emph{real} test set. FID and IS are useful for development (comparing model variants), but the decision to ship should be based on whether the generated data actually improves the system it is meant to support.
\end{practicalnote}

\subsection{Filled Example: Pedestrian Scene Generator}

\begin{example}[Generative Model Audit Card for Nighttime Pedestrian Augmentation]

\textbf{Model family.} Conditional diffusion model (U-Net backbone, 256$\times$256 resolution). Classifier-free guidance with scale $w = 5$.

\textbf{Training data.} 50,000 frames from campus shuttle cameras (72\% daytime, 25\% dusk/dawn, 3\% nighttime). Known gap: nighttime frames are underrepresented, which is the motivation for generation.

\textbf{Generation task.} Generate synthetic nighttime pedestrian scenes to augment the training set for a pedestrian detector.

\textbf{Conditioning.} Time-of-day label (day, dusk, night) and pedestrian count (0, 1, 2+).

\textbf{Quality metrics.} FID = 28.3 (all conditions), FID = 34.7 (nighttime only, compared to real nighttime frames). Feature extractor: Inception-v3 fine-tuned on campus imagery.

\textbf{Diversity assessment.} Visual inspection of 200 generated nighttime samples shows variation in pedestrian position, pose, and street lighting. However, all generated scenes use the same two camera viewpoints from the training data. Rare events (wheelchair users, cyclists) are absent from generated nighttime scenes.

\textbf{Human evaluation.} Three annotators reviewed 100 generated nighttime scenes alongside 100 real ones. Fooling rate: 68\% (annotators labeled 68\% of generated scenes as ``real''). Inter-rater agreement (Fleiss's kappa): 0.54.

\textbf{Known failure modes.} Generated pedestrians sometimes have distorted limbs at close range. Rain effects are unconvincing (training data has very few rainy nighttime frames). Text on signs and license plates is garbled.

\textbf{Downstream impact.} Adding 2,000 generated nighttime scenes to the pedestrian detector's training set improved nighttime recall from 0.62 to 0.74 on the real nighttime test set. Daytime performance was unchanged ($\pm$0.01).

\textbf{Misuse risks.} The model can generate realistic campus scenes with identifiable buildings. Safeguard: model weights are stored on a restricted server and generation is logged. Generated images are watermarked for internal use only.

\end{example}

\begin{decisionbox}
\textbf{Choosing a generative model:}
\begin{itemize}
\item If you need a structured latent space for interpolation, anomaly detection, or understanding data structure, start with a VAE.
\item If you need sharp, realistic samples and can invest in training stability, consider a GAN.
\item If sample quality is paramount and generation speed is not a bottleneck, consider a diffusion model and measure whether its diversity and conditioning behavior match the task.
\item If you need exact likelihoods (e.g., for density estimation or anomaly scoring), consider normalizing flows.
\item In all cases, evaluate on the downstream task, not just on sample quality metrics.
\end{itemize}
\end{decisionbox}

\section{Common Mistakes in Generative Modeling}

Several mistakes recur when building and deploying generative models. The first is evaluating only sample quality and ignoring diversity: a model that produces 100 beautiful but nearly identical images is useless for data augmentation. The second is trusting FID as a definitive quality measure: FID depends on the feature extractor, the sample size, and the data domain, and a low FID does not guarantee that generated data is useful for the downstream task. A third is using generated data for both training and evaluation: if synthetic data is used to augment the training set, the test set must remain real and untouched. A fourth is ignoring the failure modes of the generative model: if the model cannot generate rare events or underrepresented conditions, using its output as training data will not fix the coverage gap it was meant to address. Finally, deploying a generative model without considering misuse---realistic fake images, synthetic text used for deception---is an ethical failure that the generative model audit card is designed to prevent.

\section*{Chapter Summary}

Generative models learn to produce new data from latent representations. Variational autoencoders impose a smooth probabilistic structure on the latent space, enabling interpolation and anomaly detection but producing blurrier samples. Generative adversarial networks use adversarial training to produce sharp samples but suffer from training instability and mode collapse. Diffusion models decompose generation into iterative denoising, achieving the highest sample quality at the cost of slow generation. Evaluating generative models requires assessing both quality and diversity; automated metrics (FID, IS) are useful proxies but not definitive. Generated data should always be validated against real held-out data. The generative model audit card documents what the model generates, how it was evaluated, what failure modes exist, and what misuse risks are present.

\begin{studyquestions}
\item How does a VAE's latent space differ from a standard autoencoder's latent space?
\item What do the two terms in the VAE loss (reconstruction and KL divergence) each encourage?
\item Why does the reparameterization trick enable backpropagation through sampling?
\item Why do GANs tend to produce sharper images than VAEs?
\item What is mode collapse, and why is it hard to detect with standard training metrics?
\item How do diffusion models decompose generation into many small steps, and why does this help quality?
\item What is the quality--diversity trade-off in classifier-free guidance?
\item Why is FID an imperfect measure of generative quality?
\item When is downstream task performance a better evaluation metric than FID or IS?
\item What should a generative model audit card document about failure modes?
\end{studyquestions}

\begin{exercises}
\item \exercisetag{Implementation}{Advanced}Train a VAE on MNIST or Fashion-MNIST. Visualize the latent space (using a 2D latent code) by coloring points by their class label. Do classes form distinct regions? Sample new digits by decoding random latent points and inspect the results.
\item \exercisetag{Implementation}{Intermediate}Using the trained VAE from the previous exercise, interpolate between two digits in latent space (e.g., from a ``3'' to a ``7''). Show the decoded images at 5 equally spaced points along the interpolation path. Is the transition smooth?
\item \exercisetag{Diagnosis}{Intermediate}Compare the outputs of a VAE and a GAN trained on the same dataset. For each, generate 100 samples and assess: (a) visual quality of individual samples, (b) diversity across the 100 samples. Which model produces better individual samples? Which produces more diverse samples?
\item \exercisetag{Implementation}{Intermediate}Compute the FID between a set of generated images and a set of real images using a pre-trained feature extractor. Then compute FID using a different feature extractor. Do the two FID scores agree on which generator is better? What does this tell you about FID's reliability?
\item \exercisetag{Design}{Intermediate}Design a data augmentation experiment using a generative model of your choice. Specify: the downstream task, the generative model, the conditioning scheme, the number of generated samples, and the evaluation protocol. Explicitly state what the real held-out test set is and why the generated data must not contaminate it.
\item \exercisetag{Design}{Advanced}Construct a generative model audit card (Table~\ref{tab:gen-audit-card}) for a generative model you have trained or studied. Include at least two known failure modes and one misuse risk.
\end{exercises}

\begin{challengeexercises}
\item \exercisetag{Diagnosis}{Advanced}A team uses a GAN to augment pedestrian detection training data. After augmentation, overall detection accuracy improves by 5\%, but detection of wheelchair users gets worse. Diagnose the problem and propose a fix.
\item \exercisetag{Diagnosis}{Advanced}Explain why a diffusion model with 1000 denoising steps produces higher-quality samples than one with 20 steps, but why 20 steps may be sufficient for a given application. Under what conditions would you choose fewer steps?
\item \exercisetag{Diagnosis}{Advanced}A VAE trained on student messages has a well-organized latent space where nearby points decode to similar messages. But when you sample from the prior $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$, many decoded messages are incoherent. Explain what has gone wrong (hint: consider the gap between the aggregate posterior and the prior) and propose a mitigation.
\item \exercisetag{Design}{Advanced}Design an evaluation protocol for a text-conditioned diffusion model used to generate synthetic medical images. What metrics would you use? What human evaluation would you require? What regulatory or ethical considerations apply?
\end{challengeexercises}
