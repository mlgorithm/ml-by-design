\chapter{From Models to Systems}
\label{ch:models-systems}

A good model is not yet a good system. Once predictions enter a real workflow, the relevant question changes from whether the model is accurate to whether the service built around it is useful, stable, and governable. Inputs must arrive in the right form, thresholds become operational policies, human review creates queues and bottlenecks, and monitoring must continue after launch. This chapter asks whether the entire decision pipeline improves the real task under real organizational constraints. By the end of the chapter, the reader should be able to write a system-readiness brief that connects model quality to operational reality. A \emph{service-level objective} is a measurable promise about latency, backlog, coverage, or severe-error control, and a \emph{data contract} states what inputs, units, and preprocessing rules must hold before the service may act.

\begin{learninggoals}
\item Explain why a model metric is not enough to justify a deployed service.
\item Translate model behavior into service-level objectives, input contracts, policies, and fallback paths.
\item Design thresholds, review queues, escalation rules, and rollback triggers around operational capacity.
\item Use monitoring and ownership to keep deployed behavior governable after launch.
\item Compare language and vision systems through the same systems-readiness questions.
\item Write a system-readiness brief that connects model quality to operational reality.
\end{learninggoals}

Keep the same running case in view from the earlier language, experiments, and reliability chapters: a grounded course-support assistant that routes student messages, retrieves institutional policy, drafts bounded answers for routine cases, and escalates uncertain or high-risk cases to humans. This chapter asks whether that system is ready to launch as an actual service rather than as an impressive demo.

That language workflow remains the primary capstone because it makes contracts, retrieval, escalation, and review paths easy to see. It also gives the chapter its spine: service objective first, then requirements and contracts, then action policy, then monitoring and launch readiness. But the same systems questions should also survive outside one domain, so this chapter will repeatedly contrast it with the dashboard-camera pedestrian detector from Chapter~\ref{ch:vision}. The aim is to make the systems lesson clearly broader than educational NLP.

\section{Systems Thinking Changes the Question}

When students first learn machine learning, it is natural to think of the model as the product. In practice, the model is only one component inside a larger service. The service has users, deadlines, costs, review paths, logging requirements, failure cases, and operating assumptions. A classifier with strong test accuracy might still be unusable if it is too slow, if it produces too many low-confidence cases for human review capacity, or if it depends on features that are not available at prediction time.

\subsection{From Model Objective to Service Objective}

A model objective is usually narrow: minimize a training loss, maximize validation accuracy, reduce error on a benchmark. A service objective is broader: improve a workflow while respecting operational limits. The two are related, but they are not identical.

Consider the course-support assistant. The institution does not really want confidence scores or generated answers for their own sake. It wants a process that gives students fast, reliable help on routine questions while preserving human advising capacity for ambiguous, sensitive, or high-risk cases. If the assistant escalates too many messages, advisors cannot keep up. If it escalates too few, the system may answer questions that require human judgment or authority. If its behavior shifts every time a policy page is updated, staff will stop trusting it. Good system design begins when the modeling problem is tied back to that service objective.

\subsection{Service-Level Objectives Make the Objective Operational}

One useful way to make the service objective concrete is to write service-level objectives rather than stopping at general hopes such as ``be accurate'' or ``be helpful.'' A service-level objective states what the workflow must achieve under real operating conditions: for a support assistant, that may mean maximum response latency for routine questions, minimum escalation recall on high-risk messages, maximum average human-review backlog, minimum grounded-citation coverage on automated answers, and a maximum severe-error rate before rollback is required.

These are not replacements for model metrics. They are the bridge between model behavior and operational value. A model can raise offline answer accuracy slightly while violating the system's response-time or review-capacity objectives. In that case the model improved, but the service did not.

If the team cannot name the service-level objectives, it is still too early to say whether a threshold, model family, or deployment plan is good. The model objective becomes meaningful only when it is tied to a workflow target the organization actually cares about. Before asking which model performs best, ask what decision or workflow is being improved, what capacity exists to act on the output, and what kinds of mistakes are acceptable. That sequence is also the chapter order in miniature: state the service objective, write the input contract, choose the operating policy and fallback path, test latency and capacity limits, define monitoring and rollback triggers, and only then decide whether launch is justified. The readiness brief at the end is the written form of that workflow.

\subsection{Success Includes More Than Accuracy}

Several non-accuracy constraints routinely matter: latency, or how quickly a prediction must be returned; throughput, or how many predictions the system must handle; review capacity, or how many uncertain cases humans can inspect; cost, or what computation, storage, or annotation effort is affordable; stability, or whether outputs fluctuate enough to make users lose trust; and auditability, or whether later investigators can explain what happened.

These constraints are not secondary implementation details. They change which model class, threshold, interface, or deployment pattern is appropriate. The next section starts with the first deployment question in that workflow: what must the service achieve before any model can be called good enough?

\section{Requirements Before Training}

Well-designed systems begin with requirements. A requirement is a statement about what the system must accomplish or avoid under real operating conditions. Some requirements are quantitative, such as a maximum response time or minimum recall on a high-risk class. Others are structural, such as requiring human confirmation for severe decisions.

\begin{definition}[Operational Requirement]
An operational requirement is a concrete condition that the deployed system must satisfy, such as a latency bound, a review-capacity limit, a minimum recall target, or a mandatory fallback path.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Worked Example: Queue Capacity Changes the Threshold}

Suppose the advising team behind the course-support assistant can manually inspect at most $30$ escalated messages per day. The system assigns each incoming message an \emph{escalation score}, where larger values mean stronger reason to defer to a human. On a $200$-message day, a lower escalation threshold sends $78$ messages to humans. A stricter escalation threshold sends $26$.

If the team deploys the more aggressive policy because it appears safer in isolation, the queue may overload the staff and slow all responses, including the truly urgent ones. The seemingly more cautious threshold can therefore produce the weaker service. The stricter threshold may let more medium-risk cases stay automated, but it also produces a queue the organization can actually process.

\begin{mathlens}
As a simple day-by-day toy model, let $q_t$ be the review backlog after day $t$, let $r$ be daily human capacity, and let $a(\tau)$ be the number of cases escalated by threshold $\tau$. Then
\[
q_{t+1}=\max\{0,\;q_t+a(\tau)-r\}.
\]
In this toy recurrence, a lower escalation threshold destabilizes the queue when it makes $a(\tau)>r$, because the backlog then grows instead of clearing.
\end{mathlens}

\begin{theorem}[Little's Law links average backlog, arrival rate, and waiting time]
For a stable system in steady state, the average number of items in the system satisfies
\[
L=\lambda W,
\]
where \(L\) is the average number of items in the system, \(\lambda\) is the average arrival rate, and \(W\) is the average time an item spends in the system \citep{little1961}.
\end{theorem}

The toy backlog recurrence above is useful because it makes overload vivid one day at a time: if escalations arrive faster than they can be cleared, the backlog grows instead of settling. Little's Law adds the steady-state version of the same systems lesson: once the review process is stable, larger average backlog implies longer average waiting time because the queue itself becomes part of the service.

This is not a trick example. Many deployments face exactly this issue. The useful prediction is not the one that looks best in isolation. It is the one that leads to the best action process under real constraints.

\begin{example}[Contrast Case: Capacity Looks Different in Vision]
For a pedestrian-alert system, the same requirement may appear as braking latency or the maximum number of false alerts per hour before drivers stop trusting the system. The resource is not an advising queue, but the systems lesson is identical: operating policy must fit the real capacity of the downstream process.
\end{example}

\begin{prereqbridge}
Precision and recall describe statistical behavior. Capacity tells us whether the downstream organization can act on the predictions. A system designer must connect the two. A threshold that increases recall may still be unacceptable if it creates more positive predictions than the workflow can absorb.
\end{prereqbridge}

\subsection{Requirements Should Be Written Down Early}

Teams often delay requirement writing because it feels less exciting than training models. That is backwards. If severe errors, response-time limits, or review paths are not defined early, then later evaluation becomes ambiguous. A model cannot be ``good enough'' until the system has stated what ``enough'' means. Once those requirements are explicit, the next systems question is whether the served inputs actually satisfy the assumptions under which the model was evaluated.

\section{Data Contracts and Training-Serving Consistency}

Many system failures occur even when the model itself is reasonable. The problem is that the data seen at serving time does not match what the training pipeline assumed. Features may be computed differently, missing values may be encoded differently, text may be tokenized inconsistently, or a timestamp field may be shifted by one unit. These failures are often called \emph{training-serving skew} \citep{sculley2015}.

\begin{definition}[Training-Serving Skew]
Training-serving skew is a mismatch between the data representation or feature-processing pipeline used during model development and the one used during deployment.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Why Small Pipeline Mismatches Matter}

Modern models are sensitive to representation. A linear classifier expects features on the scale it was trained on. A tree expects the same fields and semantics it saw during fitting. A neural model expects the same tokenization, image normalization, or waveform preprocessing. If the serving pipeline silently changes one of those steps, the deployed system is effectively running a different model from the one that was evaluated offline.

\begin{mathlens}
If training standardizes a feature as $z=(x-\mu_{\text{tr}})/\sigma_{\text{tr}}$ but serving uses $\tilde z=(x-\mu_{\text{sv}})/\sigma_{\text{sv}}$, then
\[
\tilde z=\frac{\sigma_{\text{tr}}}{\sigma_{\text{sv}}}z+\frac{\mu_{\text{tr}}-\mu_{\text{sv}}}{\sigma_{\text{sv}}}.
\]
For a linear score $s=wz+b$, the served score becomes $\tilde s=w\tilde z+b$, so a changed mean or scale shifts the effective decision boundary even though the weights are unchanged.
\end{mathlens}

\subsection{Worked Example: A Retrieval Contract Bug}

Suppose the course-support assistant was evaluated with a retrieval pipeline that indexed the current semester's official policy pages, chunked them by section, and attached course and term metadata to each chunk. At serving time, a new ingestion job accidentally mixes last semester's pages with the current ones and drops part of the course metadata. The same student question about an extension now retrieves an outdated late-policy passage because the serving index no longer matches the evaluated one.

The consequences can be serious. A message that should have triggered a current-semester escalation path may now receive a confident but stale answer. Nothing is wrong with the language model weights. The system failed because the evidence contract was broken.

\begin{failurebox}
Training-serving skew is dangerous because it often leaves the model file unchanged. Teams may say ``we deployed the validated model'' even though the effective input representation at serving time is different from the one that was validated.
\end{failurebox}

\begin{example}[Real-World Callout: Hidden Technical Debt]
Sculley et al.\ argue that many machine-learning failures come not from the model alone, but from undeclared data dependencies, brittle feature pipelines, feedback loops, and glue code that quietly changes the effective system \citep{sculley2015}. Amershi et al.\ describe similar production realities in large software organizations, where data verification, configuration management, and monitoring often dominate the real engineering burden \citep{amershi2019}. The practical lesson is that a clean notebook result can still hide a brittle service.
\end{example}

\subsection{Data Contracts Reduce Ambiguity}

A practical response is to define data contracts. These specify what features exist, how they are computed, what units they use, which values are allowed to be missing, how categorical values are encoded, and what validation checks must pass before inference occurs. In mature systems, model deployment is blocked when these contracts fail. Requirements state what the service must achieve; contracts state what must be true before the model is allowed to act.

\begin{practicalnote}
Many deployment bugs are ordinary software bugs: wrong field order, missing normalization, stale joins, silently changed units, or inconsistent tokenization. Systems engineering matters because these bugs can dominate model quality in production.
\end{practicalnote}

Once the input contract is fixed, the next systems question is not what the model predicts, but how the service will act on that prediction.

\section{Prediction Is Not the Decision}

One of the most important system lessons is that a model output is not the final decision. Most models return a score, probability, rank, or generated candidate. The deployed system must still decide what action to take with that output. After the service objective, requirements, and input contract are fixed, this is the next design boundary: how does a score become an action in the real workflow?

\subsection{Thresholds Are Policies}

Choosing a threshold is not a purely mathematical cleanup step after training. It is a policy that trades off false positives, false negatives, workload, and risk. In a low-stakes recommendation setting, one threshold may be acceptable. In a high-stakes screening setting, a different threshold or a mandatory human review path may be required.

\subsection{Queueing, Review Capacity, and Human Bottlenecks}

Once abstention or escalation is available, the threshold also becomes a queueing decision. Suppose the course-support assistant handles about $120$ messages on a busy day and the advising team can review at most $20$ escalated cases before the backlog starts harming response time. A lower threshold might escalate $36$ messages, while a stricter threshold escalates $18$.

If the lower threshold improves severe-error control only slightly but creates a daily backlog of $16$ unresolved messages, then after five similar days the system accumulates roughly $80$ delayed cases. At that point the system may be statistically cautious and operationally failing. Students should recognize this as a systems error, not merely as a staffing footnote.

\begin{mathlens}
The same queue can be written as $q_{t+1}=\max\{0,\;q_t+a_t-r\}$, where $a_t$ is the day-$t$ deferral count. Stability requires average arrivals below capacity, $\mathbb{E}[a_t]<r$. If a lower escalation threshold raises deferred cases from $18$ to $36$ while $r=20$, then the expected drift becomes positive and the backlog grows.
\end{mathlens}

In the toy model, the right threshold is therefore constrained by both mistake costs and queue capacity. Under Little's Law, the same pressure reappears as waiting time: once average deferrals rise, average review delay must rise with them unless the organization adds capacity or narrows automation. A policy that looks safer on isolated examples may therefore produce a weaker service once review load, delay, and reviewer fatigue are counted as part of the outcome.

\begin{decisionbox}
When human review is part of the system, do not choose the threshold from precision and recall alone. Estimate how many cases the policy will defer per hour or per day, compare that with real review capacity, and ask what backlog the organization can tolerate before service quality degrades.
\end{decisionbox}

\subsection{Ranking Allocates Exposure}

In search and recommendation systems, the deployed action is often not ``approve or reject'' but ``which items will users actually see, and in what order?'' A ranked list allocates attention. Items shown higher receive more clicks, views, purchases, or follow-up actions partly because they were exposed earlier.

Even the running support-assistant case contains a smaller version of this issue: ranking retrieved passages changes which evidence the generator or the human reviewer sees first, so exposure logic is not only a recommender-system problem.

This makes ranking systems different from ordinary static prediction tasks. Joachims et al.\ show that clickthrough data is informative but biased by position and presentation \citep{joachims2005}. Chapter 1 made the same point from the data side for recommendation logs: observed interactions are filtered by earlier exposure decisions rather than sampled neutrally from all possible user-item pairs \citep{autodebias2021}. A deployed ranking therefore changes the future data that the next model version will see.

The practical consequence is that teams should not monitor only click-through or engagement rate. They should also monitor exposure concentration, subgroup or catalog coverage, repeated overexposure of already popular items, and whether the ranking is narrowing what users get to see. In systems language, a recommender or retrieval ranker is not merely predicting preference. It is shaping the information environment.

\subsection{Selective Prediction and Abstention}

Sometimes the right system does not force the model to answer every case. Instead, it allows abstention: confident cases are automated, while ambiguous cases are deferred to humans or alternative procedures \citep{geifman2017}. In the running support-assistant case, the real decision is often among answer, refuse, and escalate rather than simply positive versus negative.

The word \emph{confidence} needs discipline here. It should not mean a vague feeling that the model ``sounds certain.'' It should mean an estimate tied to the action policy. For the support assistant, a useful score would be something like the estimated probability that an automatic answer will stay in scope, remain grounded in approved evidence, and avoid an escalation-worthy mistake.

This score direction is deliberately different from the earlier queue examples. There the score measured escalation pressure, so a lower threshold produced more human review. Here the score measures automation safety, so a lower threshold accepts more cases for automatic answering.

\begin{mathlens}
Let $a(x)\in\{0,1\}$ indicate whether the system answers on input $x$, and let $\ell(f(x),y)$ be the loss on answered cases. Then coverage is $\phi=\mathbb{E}[a(x)]$ and selective risk is
\[
R_\phi(f)=\frac{\mathbb{E}[a(x)\,\ell(f(x),y)]}{\mathbb{E}[a(x)]}.
\]
This quantity is defined when \(\mathbb{E}[a(x)]>0\), meaning the system answers at least some cases. On this automation-safety score, lowering the threshold usually raises coverage, but it can also raise selective risk if the newly accepted cases are too uncertain. This is why thresholding works best when confidence is reasonably calibrated.
\end{mathlens}

\begin{definition}[Selective Prediction]
Selective prediction is a system design in which the model predicts only on cases where it is sufficiently confident and abstains or defers on the rest.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Worked Example: Confidence, Coverage, and Review}

Suppose the course-support assistant receives $100$ messages:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item $60$ are routine questions with strong retrieval agreement and an estimated automation-safety score above $0.90$
\item $25$ have moderate scores between $0.60$ and $0.90$ because the wording is unusual or the retrieved evidence is incomplete
\item $15$ contain conflicting signals, policy ambiguity, or high-risk language and are escalated regardless of score
\end{itemize}

If the system answers only the most confident $60$ cases automatically and defers the remaining $40$, then automation coverage is $0.60$. If the automated subset is highly accurate and well grounded, and the human review process handles the deferred cases well, this design may outperform full automation on severe-error control even though fewer cases are answered automatically.

Notice what the score means in this example. A low automation-safety score does not mean ``negative class'' in the ordinary classification sense. It means ``not safe enough to answer automatically under the current policy.''

The right question is therefore not ``can the model answer every message?'' but ``which messages should the system answer on its own, and which should trigger review or fallback?'' That is a systems question. Once that action policy is written, the next question is how the team will notice when the live system drifts away from the assumptions that made the policy look acceptable.

\begin{advancednote}
Selective prediction works best when confidence scores are reasonably calibrated. If predicted probabilities do not reflect real uncertainty well, then thresholding by confidence may not separate easy and hard cases as cleanly as expected.
\end{advancednote}

\section{Monitoring After Deployment}

Deployment is not the end of evaluation. Once a system is live, the environment changes. User behavior changes, interfaces change, sensors degrade, workload grows, and downstream policies change. Monitoring is the mechanism by which the system notices that reality has drifted away from the assumptions made during development. It is what turns pre-deployment assumptions into live checks rather than launch-time promises.

\subsection{What Should Be Monitored}

Useful monitoring often includes:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{input quality}: missing fields, schema violations, extreme values
\item \textbf{distribution change}: shifts in feature statistics or class balance
\item \textbf{prediction behavior}: score distributions, abstention rates, subgroup gaps
\item \textbf{exposure dynamics}: position bias, concentration of traffic, subgroup or catalog coverage in ranked systems
\item \textbf{operational metrics}: latency, throughput, system errors, review backlog
\item \textbf{outcome metrics}: post-deployment accuracy or task success when labels eventually arrive
\end{itemize}

For the course-support assistant, useful monitors would include policy-retrieval hit rate, citation coverage in answered messages, escalation rate by subgroup, advisor override rate, backlog length, and complaint or re-contact rate after automated replies.

\begin{warningbox}
Deployed models can create feedback loops. A recommendation system that surfaces certain content causes users to engage with it, which then becomes training data that reinforces the same recommendations. A fraud detector that blocks certain transaction patterns causes fraudsters to shift tactics, changing the input distribution. These loops mean that the model's own outputs alter the data it will be trained on next. Monitoring must watch for distributional shifts that the model itself may have caused, not only shifts from external sources.
\end{warningbox}

\subsection{Delayed Labels Make Monitoring Harder}

Many real systems do not receive immediate ground truth. A support assistant may learn that an answer was inadequate only when an advisor corrects it later, when a student asks again, when a complaint arrives, or when a periodic audit catches a policy mistake. This delay means teams must monitor proxy signals as well as eventual outcomes. A silent increase in retrieval misses, escalation backlog, or answer overrides can be an early warning even before formal labels arrive.

\begin{evidencebox}
Evidence for deployment should include a monitoring plan, alert thresholds, and a rollback path. A system without post-deployment visibility is not really under control, even if its offline evaluation was careful. Production rubrics such as the ML test score make monitoring, rollback, and ownership first-class deployment requirements rather than cleanup tasks \citep{breck2016}.
\end{evidencebox}

\subsection{Rollback and Recovery}

Good systems assume that some deployments will go wrong. They therefore include ways to disable the model, revert to an earlier version, or fall back to a safer baseline. In some settings the fallback may be human review. In others it may be a conservative rule-based heuristic. Recovery planning is part of reliability, not a sign that the model is weak. The next section asks how to generate that post-launch evidence carefully instead of exposing the full user population all at once.

\subsection{Wiring the Security Threat Card in Production}
\label{sec:security-boundaries}

Post-deployment monitoring also carries the operational half of the security story. Chapter~\ref{ch:reliability} developed the threat-modeling discipline (Section~\ref{sec:security-reliability}) and introduced the Security Threat Card. That card names, per attack class, the preventive control, the monitoring signal, and the rollback path. This subsection asks what those three fields look like inside a running production system.

\emph{Preventive controls} at the systems layer are mostly request-shaping mechanisms. Rate limits---per user, per API key, per IP---can block trivial model-extraction scraping and slow down adversarial-example search. Authenticated tiers separate anonymous query traffic from trusted workflows. Size and schema checks on uploaded documents keep obviously malformed or executable content out of the retrieval pipeline. Egress controls on tool-use systems prevent a compromised prompt from triggering a damaging external action.

\emph{Detective controls} are the monitoring signals named on each threat card, wired into the same alerting stack used for drift and SLO violations. A sudden spike in near-duplicate queries from a single caller is a signature of adversarial-example search. A sharp shift in the feedback distribution from a small coordinated cluster is a signature of feedback poisoning. A jump in guardrail overrides or in abstention rates on high-risk categories is a signature of prompt-injection bypass. An unexplained change in the retrieval corpus---new passages appearing without a corresponding reviewed update---is a signature of retrieval poisoning. The point is not to build bespoke detectors for every conceivable attack. The point is that the threat card's ``monitoring signal'' row must correspond to a metric that the deployed system actually emits.

\emph{Response paths} reuse the deployment mechanisms the chapter has already introduced. Model rollback returns the serving model to a reviewed version. Retrieval-index rollback returns the corpus to the last reviewed snapshot. Feature-store freezes prevent a suspected poisoning signal from leaking into the next retraining run. Human escalation routes queries that the system now suspects are adversarial to a reviewer. Audit logs---immutable enough to be usable after an incident---let the team reconstruct which queries, which corpus state, and which model version were in play when the attack succeeded.

\begin{practicalnote}
The cheapest pre-launch check is a simple one: for every row of the security threat card, write down the exact production metric or log that realizes the monitoring signal, the exact rollback command that realizes the response path, and the on-call runbook entry that triggers when the metric crosses threshold. Cards that name signals without wiring them to production are documentation, not defense.
\end{practicalnote}

\section{Post-Deployment Experimentation}

Launching a new model to everyone at once creates two avoidable problems. First, it maximizes the blast radius if the new system behaves badly. Second, it makes it harder to learn whether the change actually improved the workflow under real usage. A safer pattern is staged release: observe the system without acting, expose a small slice of traffic first, and expand only when the evidence remains favorable.

\subsection{Shadow Mode and Canary Release}

One common first step is \emph{shadow mode}. The new model runs on live inputs, but its outputs are logged rather than used to drive the user-facing decision. This allows the team to compare the new system with the current one under realistic traffic, latency, and data-quality conditions before users are exposed to the behavior. Shadow mode is especially useful for catching missing fields, stale joins, calibration drift, or review-load surprises that were not visible offline.

If shadow results are acceptable, the next step is often a \emph{canary release}: route a small fraction of real traffic to the new system while monitoring severe-error indicators, latency, override rate, and rollback triggers closely. The point is not to celebrate partial launch. The point is to limit damage while testing whether the deployment assumptions survive contact with production.

\subsection{Online Experiments and Guardrails}

For product changes where randomized exposure is acceptable, online controlled experiments provide the strongest evidence about whether a deployment helps real users. Kohavi et al.\ argue that trustworthy online experiments are valuable precisely because intuition and offline metrics can be misleading once a change reaches live traffic \citep{kohavi2012,kohavi2013}. In this chapter's language, an A/B test is not only a product-growth tool. It is a way to test whether a systems change improves the real workflow without silently degrading something else.

\begin{advancednote}
A/B testing assumes that assigning one user to treatment does not affect another user's outcome (the stable-unit-treatment-value assumption). This assumption breaks in networked settings: if a recommendation shown to one user affects what a second user sees (through social sharing, inventory depletion, or marketplace effects), the measured treatment effect may be biased. In such cases, cluster randomization or switchback designs are safer alternatives.
\end{advancednote}

That requires guardrails. A \emph{guardrail metric} is any live measure severe enough to block expansion or trigger rollback even when the headline metric improves. A system should not be judged only on one headline metric such as click-through rate, average response time, or overall resolution rate. A rollout scorecard should also track severe failures, subgroup disparities, complaint rate, human-review backlog, safety-rule violations, and any metric that would justify immediate rollback. A small gain on a surface metric does not justify launch if a guardrail metric gets worse.

\begin{decisionbox}
Do not ship a new model to everyone simply because offline validation improved. Ask what staged release plan will test the change safely: shadow evaluation, canary traffic, randomized comparison, guardrail thresholds, and rollback conditions.
\end{decisionbox}

\subsection{Not Every System Should Be Randomized}

Some systems are too high-stakes, too asymmetric in harm, or too constrained by policy to support ordinary online experiments. In those settings the right pattern may be shadow evaluation, restricted rollout to low-risk cases, or delayed deployment until stronger evidence is available. The systems lesson is the same either way: deployment should generate evidence deliberately, not by exposing everyone to a poorly understood change.

\section{Human-in-the-Loop Design}

Human-in-the-loop design is often misunderstood. It does not simply mean adding a human somewhere in the pipeline. The question is whether the model and the human are arranged so that the combined system is better than either one alone. In this chapter's workflow, human review is not an afterthought added to rescue a weak model. It is one of the main ways a system turns uncertain predictions into bounded actions.

\subsection{Humans Need the Right Interface}

If the system presents confusing scores, hides uncertainty, or overloads reviewers with too many cases, then human oversight may be ineffective. A reviewer needs the right information at the right time: the model score, the reason for referral, the relevant features or examples, and the allowed actions. In the support-assistant case, that means the student's message, the retrieved policy passages, the draft answer if one exists, and the rule or uncertainty trigger that caused escalation.

\subsection{Humans Also Introduce Failure Modes}

People can become over-reliant on model outputs, ignore contradictory evidence, or become inconsistent under time pressure. This is sometimes called automation bias. A badly designed interface can therefore make the combined system worse than either the human or the model on its own.

\begin{decisionbox}
Adding a human reviewer does not automatically make a system safe. The review step must have clear triggers, enough time, adequate information, and authority to override the model when necessary.
\end{decisionbox}

\section{Human-AI Interaction Design}

How a model's output is presented to humans affects decision quality as much as the model's accuracy itself. A system that shows ``87\% likely at risk'' is interpreted differently from one that recommends ``flag this student for review.'' The interface is not cosmetic; it is a design choice with measurable consequences for how users trust and act on system outputs.

\subsection{The Interface Is Part of the System}

The model produces a score or prediction. The interface turns that signal into action. For human-oversight, selective-prediction, and human-in-the-loop systems, the display format, information density, and interaction cost all reshape the effectiveness of human review. Consider a system that shows ten pieces of evidence, a confidence score, a model recommendation, and a red warning flag. Contrast it with a system that shows only the confidence number. Both rest on the same underlying model, but they will produce different human decisions and different override rates. Teams often focus on model training until launch, then delegate interface design to product teams or UI engineers. The result is that decisions about display format become a free variable, never studied as carefully as model hyperparameters.

\begin{decisionbox}
The interface design for human review is not a separate product concern. It is part of the ML system's reliability. Changes to what information is shown, how uncertainty is rendered, and how much friction exists in accepting or overriding a model recommendation measurably affect decision quality.
\end{decisionbox}

\subsection{Presenting Uncertainty}

When the model produces a confidence score or probability, should the human see it? If so, in what form? Research on risk communication shows that humans interpret the same number very differently depending on format: ``70\% likely,'' ``7 out of 10,'' and a filled bar chart convey the same probability but lead to different choices \citep{lipkus2007}. For systems that escalate uncertain cases, the question is more nuanced. If the human cannot practically distinguish between a confidence of $0.70$ and $0.85$ under time pressure, collapsing those scores into three ordered categories---\emph{confident}, \emph{uncertain}, \emph{abstain}---may produce better human decisions than displaying the raw probability. In the course-support assistant, an advisor reviewing a flagged message needs to know whether the model abstained because the retrieved evidence was weak, because the wording was unusual, or because the topic touched on high-risk categories. Showing a bare confidence number often provides less information than showing the rule or mechanism that triggered escalation.

\begin{advancednote}
Calibrated probabilities help only if the human understands their meaning. Teams sometimes display confidence scores faithfully calibrated to hold uncertainty meaning under realistic conditions, only to have those scores misinterpreted because the interface provided no scale, no reference point, or no context. User studies of whether a probability display actually improves decision-making are more informative than theoretical calibration metrics.
\end{advancednote}

\subsection{Automation Bias and Overtrust}

A persistent failure mode in human-in-the-loop systems is automation bias: humans tend to accept machine recommendations uncritically, especially under time pressure. A system that is correct $90\%$ of the time may suffer worse automation bias than a system correct only $70\%$ of the time if users expect and trust the correct one. The worse the bias, the less useful the human review step becomes. Several design patterns mitigate this risk. One is to require the human to form an independent judgment before seeing the model's recommendation. Another is to show evidence or reasoning that supports the recommendation, rather than a bare score or yes-no label. A third is to design deliberate friction into high-stakes decisions: require confirmation, ask for a written justification of an override, or require two-person sign-off on severe actions. These are not obstacles to speed. They are mechanisms to ensure the human actually reviews rather than rubber-stamps.

\begin{warningbox}
Automation bias is worse when the model is usually correct. A highly accurate model can create false confidence in the system, leading humans to stop questioning its output. In that regime, the combined human-plus-model system may be worse than either the model alone or a less automated workflow where humans have more authority.
\end{warningbox}

\subsection{Explanation and Justification}

When should the system show why it made a decision? The reflexive answer is ``always,'' in the spirit of explainability or transparency. The precise answer is more conditional. High-stakes decisions where humans are expected to understand and critique the recommendation benefit from evidence: why was this case escalated, what features or examples drove the score, what policy rule was triggered? But low-stakes, fully automated decisions that never reach a human do not benefit from explanations, and displaying them only creates compliance burden without improving outcomes. Feature attributions can mislead if they are unstable---minor input perturbations produce major changes in the attributed importance---or if they reflect the model's learned spurious associations rather than true causal drivers. The standard for explanations should be pragmatic: does showing this explanation help the human make a better decision than not showing it? If a pilot study shows that the explanation is ignored, misunderstood, or used inconsistently, remove it rather than shipping a transparency theater.

For the course-support assistant, the relevant explanation is usually context: the student's original message, the retrieved policy passages that inform the answer, and the rule or confidence trigger that caused escalation. These are direct, grounded, and actionable for the advisor. In contrast, a feature-importance vector showing that the word ``dorm'' contributed +0.3 to the score and ``scholarship'' contributed $-0.1$ is less useful for human judgment.

\subsection{Designing for Override}

A human-in-the-loop system only works if humans can realistically disagree with the model. If the interface makes it easy to accept a recommendation (one click, auto-advance) but difficult to override it (five clicks, confirmation dialog, manager approval), the interface is biased toward accepting the model, and the combined system will skew toward the model's errors. Conversely, if override is frictionless, humans may override so frequently that the model becomes a rubber stamp and the escalation signal becomes noise. The override rate is a useful diagnostic. A very low override rate may indicate automation bias or a poorly designed interface that discourages dissent. A very high override rate may indicate that the model is simply not good enough for its task, or that the escalation criteria are too loose. The design challenge is to find a rate that reflects genuine uncertainty or error, not friction or bias.

\begin{example}[Override as Monitoring Signal]
In a court-support system, suppose escalated cases have a 95\% human override rate: the human reviewer disagrees with the recommendation almost always. This suggests either that the model is poor, the escalation criteria are wrong, or the interface makes overriding easy and accepting hard. The solution is not to trust the model more. It is to investigate whether the escalation rule is filtering out meaningful cases, whether the model is calibrated to the task, or whether the interface should be redesigned to balance acceptance and override friction.
\end{example}

\subsection{Running Case: Advisor Workflow}

Return to the course-support assistant. When an advisor is asked to review a student's escalated message, should they see the model's routing recommendation first or after reading the message? Seeing the recommendation first risks automation bias; the advisor may anchor on the model's choice before forming an independent judgment. Seeing it only after reading the message preserves human autonomy but delays the information that might help the advisor decide. The solution is context-dependent: show the recommendation only after the advisor has read the student's message and the relevant policy passages, to preserve independence while still providing the model's signal in time to influence action.

In systems with very high volume and limited human capacity, there may be no time for human independence checks. The pedestrian detector from Chapter~\ref{ch:vision} illustrates this constraint. A car dashboard has perhaps 300 milliseconds to alert the driver to a hazard; there is no time for the driver to form an independent judgment before seeing the alert. In that regime, the interaction design must shift to automatic detection with human monitoring of aggregate behavior. Does the alert rate stay within expected bounds? Are there patterns of false positives that suggest retraining? Does latency stay under 100 milliseconds? Here the human role is not to review each detection but to monitor whether the overall system behavior remains trustworthy.

\section{Bandits, Online Learning, and Adaptive Systems}

The earlier monitoring sections showed how to detect when a deployed system drifts away from its development assumptions. This section asks a complementary question: when should the system adapt automatically to improve its policy, rather than waiting for a human team to retrain and redeploy? This is the explore-exploit tradeoff, a classical problem in online learning and decision-making under uncertainty.

\subsection{From Monitoring to Adaptation}

Monitoring reveals when the system is degrading. The response so far has been human-driven: the team notices a drift alert, investigates the root cause, trains a new model, tests it carefully, and rolls it out. This cycle can take days or weeks, during which the system operates with a suboptimal or failing policy. In some settings this is the right approach, especially for high-stakes decisions where any policy change should be deliberate and reviewed. In other settings---particularly low-risk, high-volume, fast-feedback scenarios---the system could adapt its behavior directly based on live feedback without waiting for human retraining. The question is not whether the system should be adaptive, but when adaptation is safe and beneficial enough to justify the complexity and risks it introduces.

\subsection{The Explore-Exploit Tradeoff}

A system that always exploits its best current policy never discovers better alternatives. A system that always explores wastes time and resources on options that are known to be suboptimal. This tradeoff is the core of the bandit problem and multi-armed bandit algorithms \citep{sutton1998}. In the language of reinforcement learning, exploration means taking an action to learn its value; exploitation means taking the action known to have the highest expected value. The tension is inherent: exploring hurts immediate performance to gain information; exploitation uses current information but may miss a better policy that appears only rarely or early in the data stream.

Consider the course-support assistant. The system currently routes messages using a fixed policy: if retrieval is weak, abstain; if high-risk rules fire, escalate; if confidence is above threshold, answer. This policy was learned once during training. An adaptive system could instead use live feedback to adjust its routing decisions. If a particular class of message---say, housing-related questions---begins to generate more overrides after deployment, the system could gradually increase the routing threshold for housing, so fewer cases are answered automatically. This adapts to the changing feedback without retraining. But if the system is too quick to adapt, it might overfit to noise in the feedback signal or change its policy based on an unusual spike rather than a genuine shift in the task.

\subsection{Contextual Bandits}

When the best action depends on features of the current example, the problem becomes a \emph{contextual bandit}. The system observes context (features of the current student message), chooses an action (answer, escalate, refuse), and observes a reward (was the action correct, was it overridden, how was it received?). Critically, it observes the outcome only for the action it chose, never for the action it did not take. This is the counterfactual problem: if the system answered when it could have escalated, we never learn what would have happened had it escalated instead. Because the training signal is always incomplete, contextual-bandit learning is harder than supervised learning, where the model sees the true label regardless of its prediction.

This partial-feedback structure makes offline policy evaluation difficult. A new routing policy might improve on the current one, but to evaluate it without deploying it, the system would need to estimate what the outcome would have been under the new policy for all the cases it decided under the old policy. This is called \emph{off-policy evaluation} and is approximate by necessity. Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments-claims}'s discussion of claim discipline applies here: even in adaptive systems, claims about policy performance must be bounded rather than treated as absolute. A system that silently adapts based on weak off-policy evaluation signals may diverge from its original design without warning.

\subsection{When Online Adaptation Is Appropriate}

Adaptive systems make the most sense in low-stakes, high-volume, fast-feedback settings. Recommendation systems are a canonical example: showing a user different movie recommendations generates immediate feedback (did they click?), the stakes are low (a bad recommendation is a minor inconvenience), and the volume is high (millions of recommendations per day). Ad placement is similar. In contrast, adaptation is usually inappropriate in high-stakes settings where exploration has real ethical costs. A production medical-treatment recommender should not randomly assign patients to different treatments as an unreviewed learning mechanism; randomized assignment belongs only inside properly governed clinical trials with equipoise, consent, monitoring, and review. Criminal-justice risk assessments should not try different scoring policies on defendants to learn which works better; the individuals are subject to the exploration. Even if the system eventually learns a better policy, the cost to those exposed during the exploration phase is unjustifiable.

There are also settings where adaptation is technically possible but poorly motivated. If feedback arrives weeks or months after the decision (as in many loan-approval or hiring systems), the training signal is too delayed to be useful. A system that adapts to feedback from decisions made six months ago is not learning the current distribution; it is chasing noise in a stale signal. In such cases, occasional full retraining with fresh data is more defensible than continuous online adaptation.

\begin{decisionbox}
Online adaptation should be chosen deliberately, not default. Ask: Are the stakes low enough that exploration has acceptable cost? Is feedback fast enough that adaptation learns the current distribution, not stale noise? Is the volume high enough that learning from live feedback is more efficient than periodic retraining? If the answer to any of these is ``no,'' keep the system in human-controlled batch retraining mode.
\end{decisionbox}

\subsection{Logging Policies and Off-Policy Evaluation}

To support both online adaptation and offline evaluation of new policies, the system must log what it did and what happened. When a message arrives, log the features, the action taken, and the outcome (whether the case was overridden, whether the answer was used, eventual audit results). This log becomes a record of the policy in effect and a source of training data for learning a better one.

Off-policy evaluation works by asking: ``Given this log of past decisions under the old policy, how well would a new policy have performed?'' The challenge is that the new policy would have taken different actions on many cases, and we do not know the outcomes of actions we did not take. Various techniques---propensity weighting, doubly robust estimation, partial-information loss---attempt to estimate new-policy performance from the partial-information log, but all are approximate and all make assumptions about the data-generation process. The role of the data scientist is to understand the direction and magnitude of bias in the evaluation, not to treat it as ground truth.

This connects directly to Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments-claims}'s emphasis on claim discipline. A system that uses off-policy evaluation to adapt its policy without bounds may drift silently from its initial design. A more cautious approach is to treat off-policy evaluation as a screening step: if a new policy looks much worse than the current one, do not deploy it. If it looks similar or slightly better, deploy in shadow mode or a limited canary release and collect direct evidence before full rollout. The system learns from live feedback, but its learning is bounded by skepticism about what offline logs can fairly claim.

\begin{example}[Bandit Algorithms in Course Support]
The course-support assistant could use a contextual-bandit algorithm to adapt its routing policy over time. For each message, the system logs the routing decision, the advisor's response (was the escalation appropriate? did the advisor override?), and eventual student feedback (did the student find the answer helpful?). A learning algorithm can use this log to estimate which routing thresholds work best for which message types. If messages about housing-related issues consistently generate low override rates and high satisfaction, the system could gradually lower its threshold for housing, answering more such cases automatically. If messages about visa issues generate frequent overrides, the system could raise its threshold, escalating more visa questions to specialists. However, the system should also occasionally explore---answer a few visa questions despite lower confidence, escalate some housing questions despite higher confidence---to test whether its beliefs about those categories are still accurate. The logging and off-policy evaluation help detect and bound drift, but they do not ensure fairness or coverage by themselves; guardrail metrics, exploration constraints, and human review are still required.
\end{example}

\section{Documentation, Ownership, and Iteration}

A system that nobody owns will decay. Inputs change, staff turnover occurs, and the assumptions that were obvious during development are forgotten. Documentation helps preserve institutional memory, but documentation alone is not enough. Teams also need clear ownership of retraining, alert handling, audit requests, and rollback decisions. This is how a launch plan becomes a maintained service rather than a one-time demo.

\subsection{What Documentation Should Capture}

Useful documentation records:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item the task definition and intended use
\item the training data sources and known limitations
\item the feature pipeline and preprocessing assumptions
\item the evaluation protocol and operating threshold
\item the staged release plan, guardrail metrics, and rollback criteria
\item subgroup, robustness, privacy, and safety results
\item the monitoring plan and fallback behavior
\end{itemize}

This is not bureaucracy for its own sake. It is part of making the system understandable, maintainable, and auditable.

\subsection{Iteration Should Be Scientific}

Teams often respond to a production issue by making a fast change and hoping for improvement. A better pattern is scientific iteration: state the failure, hypothesize the cause, change one part of the system deliberately, reevaluate, and monitor whether the change improved the right metric without creating a worse problem elsewhere.

\section{Chapter Artifact: A System Readiness Brief}

By the end of this chapter, the reader should be able to produce a one-page system readiness brief before claiming that a model is deployable. The brief is the deployment counterpart to the earlier framing memo, evaluation plan, claim sheet, and reliability review card: it asks whether those earlier decisions still hold once the model becomes a service. It also condenses the same production questions emphasized by deployment checklists such as the ML test score \citep{breck2016}.

\begin{practicalnote}
The readiness brief should not appear for the first time at the end of a project. Start it when the service objective is named, add the input contract before model selection, add the operating policy before threshold tuning, and add monitoring, ownership, and no-launch triggers before any staged release.
\end{practicalnote}

Breck et al.\ propose the ML test score as a production rubric precisely because offline accuracy is not enough to justify launch \citep{breck2016}. In this chapter's language, the readiness brief is not classroom paperwork. It is a compact no-launch gate for catching missing rollback paths, broken contracts, or absent owners before deployment turns those omissions into user-facing failures.
\end{example}

\section{When Not to Build an AI System}

The final systems lesson is restraint. Not every decision problem should become a machine-learning deployment. A strong readiness process must allow ``do not launch'' as a technical conclusion rather than treating deployment as the default reward for finishing a model.

\subsection{Simple Rules Sometimes Win}

If a stable rule solves the task well, then adding ML may only increase complexity, opacity, and maintenance burden. This is especially true when the input space is narrow and the desired behavior is already well specified.

\subsection{The Organization May Lack the Needed Support}

Even a promising model should not be deployed if the surrounding organization lacks feature quality checks, review capacity, monitoring, or ownership. In that case the limiting factor is not algorithmic performance. It is system readiness.

\subsection{High Stakes Raise the Evidence Bar}

Some settings demand stronger evidence, stronger fallback behavior, or stronger privacy protection than a team can currently provide. In those settings, the correct decision may be to delay deployment, narrow the use case, or reject the system entirely.

\section{Worked System Walkthrough: Course Support}

To make the full chapter concrete, consider the grounded course-support pipeline developed across the earlier language, experiments, and reliability chapters. The walkthrough follows the same order as the readiness brief so that the chapter ends by assembling one coherent launch argument rather than introducing a separate example.

\subsection{Step 1: Define the Service Objective}

The institution's goal is not simply to maximize answer accuracy. It wants to help students get fast, reliable help on routine questions while reserving human attention for ambiguous, sensitive, or high-risk cases. The service succeeds only if response time improves, advisor workload stays manageable, and severe escalation misses are kept acceptably low.

\subsection{Step 2: Define the Input Contract}

The system receives the student's message text, course or program context when available, the current term, and a validated set of approved policy documents. Each field has a missing-value rule, format requirement, and freshness check. If the current-semester policy pages are stale, a course identifier is absent, or a document source fails validation, the system should not quietly answer anyway.

\subsection{Step 3: Train and Evaluate the Components}

The team evaluates routing, retrieval, and answer quality chronologically rather than randomly, because the real task predicts future student traffic from past semesters. It reports subgroup metrics, calibration or confidence quality, citation quality, and robustness under wording shift, document reorganization, and missing-context scenarios.

\subsection{Step 4: Choose the Action Policy}

The system does not answer every message. It answers only when the request is in scope, the retrieved evidence comes from approved current sources, the confidence is high enough, and no escalation rule fires. Messages with policy ambiguity, weak evidence, unusual phrasing, or severe-risk content are routed to human staff. This policy is chosen because advising capacity is limited and because ambiguity is exactly where human authority matters.

\subsection{Step 5: Design the Review Interface}

When the system escalates a case, the advisor should not receive only a bare risk score. The review interface should show the student's message, the retrieved policy passages, the draft answer if one was prepared, the uncertainty or rule trigger, and the permitted actions. Otherwise the review step becomes a bottleneck or a rubber stamp rather than a real safeguard.

\subsection{Step 6: Monitor and Iterate}

After deployment, the team monitors document freshness, retrieval hit rate, subgroup escalation rates, advisor override frequency, backlog growth, and eventual quality audits. If the percentage of escalated cases rises sharply, that may indicate drift, a source-ingestion bug, or a confidence-calibration failure. The team can then investigate before performance collapses.

This walkthrough shows the chapter's central point: the useful system is not just the language model or retriever. It is the model plus contracts, routing policy, review interface, monitoring, and organizational ownership.

\subsection{Contrast Case: A Pedestrian-Alert Service}

The same readiness questions survive in the dashboard-camera pedestrian detector from Chapter~\ref{ch:vision}. The service objective is no longer ``answer routine questions quickly.'' It is ``surface likely pedestrian hazards early enough to support a safe braking or alerting decision without flooding the driver or controller with false alarms.'' The input contract includes camera calibration, frame rate, lens geometry, synchronization, and health checks for glare or dropout. The operating policy might require agreement between detection and short-term tracking before a high-confidence alert is issued, with conservative slowdown behavior when the visual evidence degrades.

Monitoring also looks different without changing the systems logic. Useful signals include audited night-rain miss rate on periodically labeled clips, alert rate by lighting condition, camera-health failures, latency spikes, and disagreement between the detector and downstream tracker. A no-launch trigger might be unacceptable failure on close-range pedestrians under dusk, glare, or unseen-camera slices even if average daytime metrics remain strong.

A concrete operating rule might be: if the audited night-rain miss rate exceeds the safety threshold for three consecutive labeled evaluation windows, the service enters a conservative fallback mode and opens an incident for the engineering team. Any threshold change should go through reviewed shadow or canary evaluation before expansion, because lowering the detection threshold can increase false alerts and change downstream control behavior. Because those labels arrive with delay, the live service should also watch immediate proxy signals such as tracker disagreement, close-range near-miss reports, and camera-health failures; if those degrade sharply before the audit catches up, the driver-assistance module should reduce maximum speed until conditions improve or a human operator reviews the situation.

The details differ from the support assistant, but the readiness brief still asks the same questions about requirements, contracts, fallback behavior, monitoring, and ownership.

\section{Production Infrastructure: Making Models Servable}
\label{sec:production-infra}

Building a model that works on a validation set is not the same as running a model reliably in production. The gap between ``the model is accurate'' and ``the system is operational'' is filled by infrastructure: feature stores, model registries, and inference optimization. This section introduces these concepts at a design level, not as tooling tutorials.

\subsection{Feature Stores and Feature Freshness}

A \emph{feature store} is a centralized system that computes, stores, and serves features consistently across training and inference. The core problem it solves is \emph{training-serving skew}: the features used at training time must be computed identically at serving time, or the model will see inputs it was not trained on.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Offline features.} Computed in batch (e.g., a student's average submission time over the past semester). These change slowly and can be precomputed.
\item \emph{Online features.} Computed in real time (e.g., a student's login count in the past hour). These require streaming computation and low-latency serving.
\item \emph{Feature freshness.} How recently the feature was computed matters. A ``number of late submissions this week'' feature that is 3 days stale is not the same feature the model was trained on. Feature freshness requirements should be documented in the data contract.
\end{itemize}

\begin{practicalnote}
Before building a feature store, ask: do I actually have training-serving skew? If your training pipeline and serving pipeline compute features with the same code on the same data, a feature store adds complexity without benefit. Feature stores are most valuable when features are shared across multiple models, when feature computation is expensive (so caching matters), or when the training and serving environments are different (e.g., Python for training, Java for serving).
\end{practicalnote}

\subsection{Model Registry and Version Control}

A \emph{model registry} tracks which model version is deployed, what data it was trained on, what hyperparameters were used, and what evaluation metrics it achieved. It serves the same purpose as version control for code, but for models.

Key capabilities:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Versioning.} Every trained model gets a unique version identifier. Rolling back to a previous version should be a one-step operation.
\item \emph{Metadata.} Each version records: training data date range, hyperparameters, evaluation metrics (on validation and test sets), and the code commit that produced it.
\item \emph{Promotion workflow.} Models move through stages: ``experimental'' $\to$ ``staging'' $\to$ ``production.'' Promotion requires passing a checklist (e.g., evaluation metrics exceed thresholds, reliability review card is filled, no regressions on critical slices).
\item \emph{Lineage.} Track which training data, feature definitions, and preprocessing steps produced each model. If a data source is discovered to contain errors, lineage lets you identify all affected models.
\end{itemize}

\begin{warningbox}
Without a model registry or equivalent versioned release record, the question ``which model is running in production?'' has no reliable answer. Teams that deploy models by copying files to a server inevitably lose track of versions, deploy untested models, or cannot reproduce results. The underlying requirement is not a particular vendor tool; it is traceability from deployed model to code, data, evaluation, approval, and rollback path.
\end{warningbox}

\subsection{Inference Efficiency: Distillation, Quantization, and Pruning}

A model that takes 500ms to produce a prediction may be too slow for a real-time system, too expensive to run at scale, or too large to deploy on an edge device. Three families of techniques reduce inference cost:

\textbf{Knowledge distillation.} Train a smaller ``student'' model to mimic the predictions of a larger ``teacher'' model. The student learns from the teacher's soft predictions (probability distributions over classes), which contain more information than hard labels. The student is smaller and faster but approximates the teacher's behavior.

\textbf{Quantization.} Reduce the numerical precision of the model's weights and activations (e.g., from 32-bit floating point to 8-bit integers). This reduces memory usage and speeds up computation, especially on hardware with efficient integer arithmetic. Post-training quantization requires no retraining; quantization-aware training fine-tunes the model to compensate for precision loss.

\textbf{Pruning.} Remove weights, neurons, or entire layers that contribute little to the model's output. Structured pruning (removing entire channels or attention heads) produces models that are directly faster on standard hardware. Unstructured pruning (zeroing individual weights) produces sparse models that require specialized hardware or software to realize speed gains.

\begin{example}[Inference Optimization for Pedestrian Detection]
The campus pedestrian detector runs on an edge device in each shuttle. The full model (ResNet-50 backbone) takes 120ms per frame---too slow for 30fps real-time operation. The team applies three optimizations: (1) knowledge distillation to a MobileNet-v2 student (30ms, 2\% accuracy drop), (2) INT8 quantization (18ms, additional 0.5\% accuracy drop), and (3) structured pruning of the least-important channels (14ms, additional 0.3\% drop). The final model runs at 14ms per frame (71fps), well within the real-time budget, with a total accuracy drop of 2.8\% from the original---an acceptable trade-off documented in the system's readiness brief.
\end{example}

\subsection{Cost-Aware Serving}

Running ML models in production costs money: compute, memory, network, and storage. Cost-aware serving makes these trade-offs explicit:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Cascade architectures.} Use a cheap, fast model (e.g., a logistic regression filter) to handle easy cases and a more expensive model (e.g., a neural network) only for hard cases. The cheap model handles 80\% of traffic; the expensive model handles 20\%. Total cost drops while accuracy on hard cases is preserved.
\item \emph{Batching and caching.} Batch multiple inference requests together to exploit GPU parallelism. Cache predictions for repeated inputs (e.g., the same resource page viewed by many students). Both reduce per-request cost.
\item \emph{Auto-scaling.} Scale compute resources based on demand. A course-support system needs more capacity during midterm and finals weeks than during breaks. Over-provisioning wastes money; under-provisioning degrades latency.
\end{itemize}

\begin{decisionbox}
\textbf{Inference efficiency checklist:}
\begin{itemize}
\item What is the latency budget for a single prediction? (e.g., $<$50ms for real-time, $<$1s for batch)
\item What is the cost budget per prediction? (e.g., $<$\$0.001 for high-volume serving)
\item Can a cascade architecture route easy cases to a cheaper model?
\item Has distillation, quantization, or pruning been evaluated, and is the accuracy trade-off acceptable?
\item Is the serving infrastructure auto-scaled to match demand patterns?
\end{itemize}
\end{decisionbox}

\section{Common Mistakes in Systems Work}

\begin{mistakebox}
\item \textbf{Treating deployment as a final packaging step.} Deployment is part of the design problem from the beginning.
\item \textbf{Optimizing the model while ignoring the workflow.} A slightly better benchmark score may be irrelevant if the system cannot be run or reviewed safely.
\item \textbf{Ignoring training-serving skew.} A mismatch between training and production preprocessing can break an otherwise good model.
\item \textbf{Equating confidence with correctness.} Confidence only helps when scores are calibrated and fallback exists.
\item \textbf{Deploying without monitoring and ownership.} A system without alerts, rollback, and owners is not robust enough for long-term use.
\item \textbf{Shipping to everyone at once.} Expansion should earn its way through shadow evaluation or canary traffic.
\end{mistakebox}

\section{Closing Perspective}

This book began with problem framing and ends with system framing. That is a deliberate arc. Learning algorithms matter, but they are not the whole discipline. A mature understanding of AI connects representation, optimization, evaluation, reliability, and deployment into one coherent practice. The framing memo from Chapter 1, the evaluation plan, the claim sheet, the reliability review card, and the system readiness brief are not separate bureaucratic forms. They are the same discipline viewed at progressively more realistic levels, from defining the task to deciding whether the full service should exist at all. The final professional standard is not ``can I train a model?'' It is ``can I build a system that improves the real task under the real constraints without hiding its limitations?''

It is worth returning to the framing memo from Chapter~\ref{ch:framing-learning-problems}. Does the system readiness brief confirm, narrow, or reject the original vision? If the answer is ``narrow,'' that is usually a sign of honest engineering: the deployed system does less than the initial ambition, but what it does, it does reliably and with bounded claims.

For instance, the framing memo for the course-support assistant proposed answering all student inquiries. After evaluation, the readiness brief may \emph{narrow} this scope: the system is ready for routine policy questions (deadlines, prerequisites, grade scales) where retrieval accuracy is high and stakes are low, but high-risk or novel questions---appeals, mental health concerns, academic misconduct---must be escalated to a human advisor. The framing memo is not wrong; it is refined by evidence.

\section*{Chapter Summary}

A real AI system includes more than a model: it includes contracts, policies, interfaces, queues, monitoring, staged release, and ownership. Production infrastructure---feature stores, model registries, inference optimization, and cost-aware serving---bridges the gap between a working model and a reliable service. System quality depends on whether those pieces work together under real latency, workload, and failure constraints. The final professional standard is not model performance in isolation, but whether the whole service improves the task safely and sustainably.
